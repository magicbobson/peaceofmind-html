Pensions - Your circumstances

The value of pensions and the income they produce can fall as well as rise. You may get back less than you invested.

Transferring out of a final salary pension is unlikely to be in the best interest of most people.

Many people view pensions as being a grudge purchase, and will arrive at any number of reasons to avoid taking any action. There is also a large amount of bad publicity about pensions mentioned regularly in the media and press. In my experience this causes a lot of confusion in people’s minds, and creates mistrust in pensions. This is unfortunate because many people are missing out on good investment growth and important tax relief too. Many people are sure they would rather invest in property thinking this to be a better investment – but over recent years this has not been the case. If you have a look at the performance of an average Balanced Managed fund, you will be likley to conclude that the results look better than property value increases. Also now, the Pension Freedoms rules mean you can control how and when you take your money back out. It’s no longer true that getting the money back out has to be poor value or restrictive.

In reality a personal pension is just a savings vehicle that the government will add 20% or 40% of your contributions too (depending on how much tax you pay). There is no better or more tax efficient way to save for your retirement. Clearly paying into a pension and looking after your own interests is a very good idea. But as there is no compulsion to do so, the retirement prospects for many will be bleak.

Large numbers of people have money in very old-style pensions, some going back as far as the 1980’s. This can mean that money is left to dwindle in increasingly unsuitable, old, expensive, inflexible pension contracts.

The key to the success of a pension lies in how well the underlying investment funds perform. A pension taken out in the 1980’s may have been suitable at the time, but things may change even after a couple of years. For example:

  • Your attitude to investment risk may change meaning that the pension funds used may no longer be suitable for you.  Your changing personal circumstances and age will have an affect on this.
  • The investment funds you use may now be performing poorly, whereas once they had an excellent reputation. This often happens where there is a change of fund manager.
  • The economy and investment prospects generally can change.  You may need to take stock of this in light of your own circumstances, and alter the way your pension is invested.
  • Your pension provider may close to new business, meaning that service, fund performance and investment choice suffers.
  • You may be in a with-profits fund paying very low bonuses.  Some haven’t paid any bonus since 2003 and so are being eroded by inflation.  You may be prepared to take more risk than this, instead preferring better growth prospects.
  • You may not ever have had your Attitude to risk assessed, and you may be locked into a poorly performing with-profits fund. Some Co-op CIS, Pearl, Royal London and Phoenix with-profits funds can at times be particularly unsuited to the policyholders. The Aviva Pension Assured Fund is also quite common, and could be unsuitable unless the policy holder needs prefers a low risk, and is prepared for correspondingly low returns.
  • Your old provider may charge unrealistically high charges in comparison to the modern pensions of today.
  • You may have acquired several older pensions that you’d like to transfer to just one properly reviewed and managed pension. This will keep things more simple and easy to manage.

I can transfer your old pensions into just one modern low cost plan. The new plan will have access to thousands of Investment Funds, and be able to provide Flexi Access Drawdown. One inportant new feature is that of “Nominee Drawdown”. If you were to die early this would allow your husband/wife or children to receive the money into a completley tax free pension environment to take whenever they like, free of Inheritance Tax. This is a considerably better option than being offered a return of the money by cheque, outside of a tax free wrapper, and with no investment funds for future growth.

If you don’t have a pension yet, I can set one up for you. More importantly however I will speak to you regularly to discuss your changing circumstances and look at how your pension is performing. If changes need to be made to the funds you use I will arrange this for you.

Clearly you can’t always be in the very best fund, but my aim is to make sure your fund will perform above the average for the sector. If it doesn’t I will recommend a fund switch at our next review.
